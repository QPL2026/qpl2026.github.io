      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">A Complete and Natural Rule Set for Multi-Qudit Clifford Circuits in All Odd Prime Dimensions</td>
      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">We present a complete set of rewrite rules for multi-qudit Clifford circuits, where \emph{qudit} denotes a $d$-level quantum system with $d$ an odd prime. Completeness means that any two Clifford circuits representing the same linear map can be transformed into each other using these rules. In total, there are $19$ \emph{Clifford relations}, each involving at most three qudits and admitting an intuitive interpretation.<br><br>Our approach leverages the isomorphism between the symplectic group $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{Z}_d)$ and the quotient of the Clifford group by the Pauli group. We first derive a complete set of \emph{symplectic relations} for $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{Z}_d)$, and then lift them to Clifford relations by incorporating Pauli corrections. To do this, we introduce a \emph{symplectic normal form} that captures the stabiliser tableau of a Clifford operator and is unique up to Pauli correction. This simplification enables a streamlined derivation of a complete set of $66$ relations, which we further compress to $18$ symplectic relations. Our computations in $\mathrm{Sp}(2n, \mathbb{Z}_d)$ are formalised in the Agda proof assistant, providing a machine-verified proof of correctness.</td>

      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">A higher-order perspective on quantum signal processing</td>
      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Two distinct paradigms for quantum functional programming have been developed in the last few years: Quantum Signal Processing (QSP)-based methods, including the Quantum Singular Value Transformation (QSVT) [GSLW19], and methods based on higher-order transformations, such as the Universal Hamiltonian Eigenvalue Transformation (UHET) [OKTM25]. While UHET performs functional transformations of Hamiltonian dynamics, its relationship to QSP-based techniques has remained unclear despite evident structural similarities. In this work, we resolve this gap by establishing a connection between UHET and QSP-based frameworks, specifically, we show that UHET can be interpreted as a (randomised) linearisation of Generalised QSP (GQSP) [MW24]. Building on this result, we introduce a linearised variant of QSVT, which we call Universal Hamiltonian Singular Value Transformation (UHSVT). This algorithm enables a transformation by any sufficiently differentiable function f of any arbitrary matrix A encoded in a block of a Hamiltonian, whose dynamics is accessible as a black box, and it can be performed in a wider range of settings than previous QSVT-based approaches.<br><br>[GSLW19] A Gilyén, Y Su, G H Low, N Wiebe, STOC, 2019.<br>[OKTM25] T Odake, H Kristjánsson, P Taranto, M Murao, PRR, 2025.<br>[MW24] D Motlagh, N Wiebe, PRX Quantum, 2024.       <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">A real matrix theory consistent with both the postulates and predictions of quantum theory</td>
      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Recent results show that the statistical predictions of so-called real quantum theory (RQT) can differ from those of standard quantum theory (QT). In RQT, one restricts the Hilbert-space formalism to operators represented by real matrices, while still allowing arbitrarily large matrix dimensions. Under this restriction, there exist device-independent tests capable of distinguishing RQT from unrestricted QT.<br><br>In this work, however, we shift the focus from experimental distinguishability back to the more fundamental question: are complex numbers intrinsically necessary in quantum theory? Before considering whether RQT can be experimentally falsified, one should first ask whether it is the only consistent way to formulate a quantum-like theory over real Hilbert spaces. Even if RQT is ruled out experimentally, this would not by itself establish the necessity of complex numbers unless every other viable real alternative were also shown to be falsifiable.<br><br>We argue that the situation is more subtle. There exists a formulation—what we call real-number quantum theory (RNQT)—in which the complex Hilbert-space formalism of QT is represented entirely in terms of real matrices while remaining fully consistent with the standard postulates of real Hilbert-space quantum theory. By construction, RNQT reproduces exactly the same outcome probabilities as standard QT and is therefore empirically indistinguishable from it.<br><br>One might object that RNQT is merely “quantum theory in disguise,” since it encodes an intrinsic complex structure within a real representation. To address this concern, we show that RNQT can be embedded into a larger theoretical framework that is not in bijective correspondence with standard QT, thereby demonstrating that it is not simply a trivial reformulation.<br><br>Technically, we construct RNQT as a theory of real symmetric matrices that satisfies the formal postulates of quantum theory while reproducing all probability distributions predicted by the standard complex (Hermitian) formulation. We then extend this construction to a generalised probabilistic theory (GPT) based on the dihedral group of order 8.<br><br>Our results highlight that the supposed dichotomy between QT and RQT is misleading. The necessity of complex numbers is tied specifically to the conventional Hilbert-space formulation, rather than to quantum theory in a broader operational sense. Beyond its relevance to the QT/RQT debate, our construction is also of independent interest as a novel example of a generalised probabilistic theory.</td>

      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">The ZX-calculus and stabiliser formalism are two ubiquitous multi-tools in quantum computation.       <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">A unified framework for Bell inequalities from continuous-variable contextuality</td>
      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Although the original EPR paradox was formulated in terms of position and momentum, most studies of these phenomena have focused on measurement scenarios with only a discrete number of possible measurement outcomes. Here, we present a framework for studying non-locality that is agnostic to the dimension of the physical systems involved, allowing us to probe purely continuous- variable, discrete-variable, or hybrid non-locality. Our approach allows us to find the optimal Bell inequality for any given measurement scenario and quantifies the amount of non-locality that is present in measurement statistics. This formalism unifies the existing literature on continuous- variable non-locality and allows us to identify new states in which Bell non-locality can be probed through homodyne detection. Notably, we find the first example of continuous-variable non-locality that cannot be mapped to a CHSH Bell inequality. Moreover, we provide several examples of simple hybrid DV-CV entangled states that could lead to near-term violation of Bell inequalities.</td>

      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Algebraic techniques for photonic state preparation and characterization</td>
      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Multimode multiphoton states are at the center of many photonic quantum technologies, from photonic quantum computing to quantum sensing. Despite their wide use and apparent simplicity, the vast majority of states belonging to this class require ad-hoc procedures to be prepared and their entanglement properties remain poorly understood. Leveraging on the recent tool of stellar polynomials, we first provide a classification of the entanglement properties of such states. Those which can be generated by factorized multimode Fock states and interferometers belong to the class of passive-separable states and their entanglement is fully Gaussian in essence. those which require consecutive photon additions in non-orthogonal modes and finally those which can&#x27;t be generated by any such scheme, thereby outlining the notion of algebraic mode-intrinsic entanglement. Furthermore, we describe a procedure to prepare any such state using only interferometers, single photon additions and photon counters in a predictable number of steps. The proposed protocol relies on a map between stellar polynomials and symmetric tensors, which can then be decomposed using known results from algebraic geometry. By doing so, we uncover a mechanism of photon catalysis, where extra photons have to be added to mediate the non-Gaussian entanglement and then retrieved by post-selection on a photon-counter outcome. Moreover, we describe a mapping between such a procedure and boson sampling or Gaussian boson sampling. We also compare our protocol to state-of-the-art alternatives tailored to very specific subclasses of states, finding comparable probabilities of success, with the added benefit that we are never approximating the target state, but rather exactly generating it.</td>

      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">We present quantum circuits for comparison and increment operations that achieve an asymptotically optimal gate count of O(n) and depth of O(log n) over the Clifford+Toffoli gate set, while using a provably minimal number of qubits.       <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Basis-independent stabilizerness and maximally noisy magic states</td>
      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Absolutely stabilizer states are those that remain convex mixtures of stabilizer states after conjugation by any unitary. Here we give a characterization of such states for multiple qudits of all prime dimensions by introducing a polytope of their allowed spectra. We illustrate this through the examples of one qubit, two qubits, and one qutrit. In particular, the set of absolutely stabilizer states for a single qubit is a ball inscribed in the stabilizer octahedron, but for higher dimensions the geometry is more complicated. For odd-prime-dimensional qudits, we also give a complete characterization of absolutely Wigner-positive states, i.e., states whose Wigner function remains nonnegative after conjugation by any unitary. In so doing, we show there are absolutely Wigner-positive states that are not absolutely stabilizer, which can be seen as a unitarily-invariant version of bound magic. We then study the radii of the largest balls contained in the sets of absolutely stabilizer states and absolutely Wigner-positive states. These radii respectively tell us the lowest possible purity of non-stabilizer and Wigner-negative states. Conversely, we also find the radius of the smallest ball containing the set of absolutely Wigner-positive states, giving a tight purity-based necessary condition thereof.</td>

      <td style="vertical-align: top; padding: 8px;">Classically simulating circuits with bosonic codes is challenging due to the prohibitive cost of simulating quantum systems with many, possibly infinite, energy levels. We propose an algorithm to simulate circuits with encoded Gottesman-Kitaev-Preskill (GKP) states, specifically for odd-dimensional encoded qudits. Our approach is tailored to be especially effective in the most challenging but practically relevant regime, where the codeword states exhibit high (but finite) squeezing.
